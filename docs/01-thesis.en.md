# 01 · Thesis and Theoretical Grounding

[한국어](01-thesis.md) | **English**

## Starting point — a collision of two user species

This space has two kinds of users. An AI agent needs neither gravity nor mediating transitions between spaces.
A human needs correspondence with real space, and mediating thresholds.
The collision — one virtual space cannot satisfy both demands at once — was the starting point of the design.

The resolution came from the library — ordinary users and librarians face the same shared data, books,
in different ways. Same data, different interfaces. In this project the human rooms (Reading Room, Desk,
Director's Office) carry mediation and rituals of entry; the system rooms (Cataloguing Room, Stacks)
are entered directly through nodes of the diagram, without them.

## The two axes

The argument of this project rests on two axes.

**Axis 1 — Asymmetric Visibility Hierarchy.**
Who can see whom becomes a design variable.

| Relation | Visibility | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Worker ↔ Worker | **Invisible** | Blocks conformity pressure and collusion. Data purity. |
| Worker → Supervisor | Visible | The review function. |
| Supervisor → Director | Visible | Hierarchical accountability. |
| Agent ↔ Human | Visible | Explainability. |

When every participant can see the hierarchy *itself*, each one's sense of responsibility rises.
The worker knows they are observed, the supervisor is conscious of the duty of review, and the director
feels the weight of the final decision. The panopticon's vertical surveillance **horizontalized** —
an infrastructure of mutual accountability, not surveillance.

**Axis 2 — Everyday spatial grammar as an infrastructure of trust.**
The internal structure of an AI system is invisible, and therefore hard to trust.
This project borrows the grammar of a space everyone knows bodily — the library
(reading room, desk, stacks, director's office) — and gives each layer of the system
*position, distance, and visibility*. Whoever knows the grammar of the space
does not need to learn the grammar of the system.

## Theoretical grounding

**Shannon Mattern — "Library as Infrastructure" (2014), "A City Is Not a Computer" (2021).**
Mattern criticizes the habit of metaphorizing digital systems as *platforms*. The platform metaphor is flat
like a two-dimensional stage, concealing the infrastructural depth beneath. Her alternative is the *library* —
a deep space where reading rooms, stacks, and librarians' offices each hold different roles.
This project is a case of carrying that theoretical proposal into an actual interface.

**Bill Hillier — Space Syntax.** Hierarchy is expressed not by color or label but by *topology*
(depth, integration). The director's office lies deepest; the reading room shallowest.

**Sou Fujimoto — intimate exterior.** Sight passes through while territories stay separate.
The "shadows beyond blurred glass" of the worker's screen — knowing someone is beside you
without knowing what they do — is precisely this.

**Engawa (縁側) · Ma (間) · Oku (奥).** Mediation between inside and outside (engawa), the activeness
of emptiness (ma), staged entry into depth (oku). Asymmetric visibility sits where Fujimoto's intimate exterior
meets Hillier's space syntax — a point where Western spatial theory and Japanese architectural concepts
say the same thing.

**The safety engineering of mediation.** Circuit breakers after the 2010 Flash Crash, the paradox of
aviation automation, Norman's forcing functions, the Swiss Cheese Model.
Inserting *calibrated delay* into an automated system is not inefficiency but a safety device
that creates the conditions for awareness, review, and intervention.
In this project, mediation is expressed as the time of entry transitions.

## Open question — System-as-space vs. Designed space

A question raised in review: is this work *describing* a system through spatial metaphor (system-as-space),
or is it a *designed space* in which spatial properties become variables of the system?

The current working hypothesis is a third direction —
**"a space in which the visitor moves between roles and comes to know, bodily, what is visible and what is not from each position."**
Taking the asymmetry of visibility itself as the design variable.
